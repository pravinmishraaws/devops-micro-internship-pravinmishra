## Your Article

Five years earlier, Tim Obi was an engineering student completing his SIWES internship while trying to balance school responsibilities with a demanding DevOps Micro Internship (DMI). At the time, he had little experience with cloud computing, Linux, automation, or DevOps practices. What he possessed instead was something far more valuable—a willingness to learn consistently, remain disciplined, and embrace challenges without giving up.

That decision became the turning point of his career.

Rather than treating DMI as just another online course, Tim approached it as a commitment to transform the way he learned. Every week, he built projects, documented his progress publicly, and published detailed reflections about the lessons he learned. Those reflections eventually became a record of his growth and inspired many aspiring engineers who were beginning similar journeys.

His GitHub profile steadily evolved into a portfolio that demonstrated far more than completed assignments. It showcased infrastructure automation projects, Linux scripting, cloud deployments, CI/CD pipelines, Docker containers, Kubernetes clusters, and Infrastructure as Code. Every repository reflected another challenge solved and another skill mastered.

As his technical skills grew, so did his professional opportunities.

After graduating from university, Tim earned industry-recognized certifications in AWS Cloud, Docker, Kubernetes, Terraform, and Linux Administration. Those certifications strengthened the practical experience he had already developed through projects, making him a competitive candidate in the DevOps industry.

He later joined a technology company as a DevOps Engineer, where he contributed to designing reliable cloud infrastructure, automating software deployment pipelines, improving monitoring systems, and reducing deployment times through automation. His work enabled development teams to deliver software faster and more reliably, while improving system stability for thousands of users.

Beyond his technical responsibilities, Tim became known for his collaborative mindset. Colleagues respected him not simply because of his technical knowledge, but because he willingly shared what he knew, mentored junior engineers, and encouraged a culture of continuous learning. The lesson he first learned during DMI—that asking questions and collaborating with others accelerates growth—became one of the defining principles of his career.

His commitment to learning never slowed down. He regularly published technical articles explaining DevOps concepts in simple terms, maintained a personal portfolio website that documented his projects, and remained active on GitHub by contributing to open-source initiatives. Through workshops, online communities, and mentoring sessions, he led initiatives that helped aspiring engineers gain confidence and practical experience in cloud computing and DevOps.

The financial rewards followed naturally, but they never became his primary measure of success. What mattered most was the impact he created through his work, the people he mentored, and the systems he helped build. His career became proof that consistency, curiosity, and discipline often outperform raw talent.

Looking back, the transformation never came from a single breakthrough or one extraordinary opportunity. It came from hundreds of ordinary days spent learning after work, completing projects when motivation was low, asking for help when he reached his limits, and refusing to let procrastination define his future.

The young engineering student who once wondered whether he could succeed in technology had become a respected DevOps professional whose journey reminded others that remarkable careers are rarely built overnight. They are built one disciplined day at a time.

## Answer

There have been times when I took shortcuts instead of putting in my best effort. One example was delaying important work until the last minute and then trying to finish it as quickly as possible instead of giving it the attention it deserved. Although I completed the task, I didn't feel proud of the result. I mostly felt regret because I knew I was capable of producing something much better. Since then, I have been making a conscious effort to plan ahead, stay disciplined, and complete tasks honestly, even when it requires more time and effort.

# Reflection – Week 1

### Biggest insight I got about myself this week

This week, I realized that I am capable of learning difficult things as long as I stay consistent and keep showing up, even when the process feels overwhelming. Before joining DMI, I often believed I had to figure everything out on my own. However, while working through the assignments, I got stuck several times and had to reach out to fellow members of my DMI Group 2 for help. That experience showed me that collaboration is just as important as consistency. Asking for help didn't make me less capable—it helped me learn faster and understand concepts better. I also discovered that building systems and following a structured routine is far more effective than relying on motivation alone. This week taught me that growth comes from combining discipline with the willingness to learn from others.

### My biggest weakness/loop I noticed

One weakness I noticed this week is my tendency to procrastinate. Although I was committed to completing the assignments, I kept postponing them and ended up working under pressure on the very last day before the submission deadline. This made the experience more stressful than it needed to be and reduced the time I had to review my work properly. I realized that procrastination doesn't save time—it only creates unnecessary pressure and affects the quality of my learning. Going forward, I want to break this habit by following the weekly routine I created and completing tasks earlier instead of waiting until the deadline.

### One system I will implement from this week (exact habit + time)

From Monday to Friday, I will dedicate 2 hours every evening (7:00 PM – 9:00 PM) to DMI learning and assignments immediately after returning from my SIWES placement. I will break each week's assignments into smaller daily tasks instead of waiting until the deadline. If I get stuck for more than 30 minutes on a problem, I will ask questions in my DMI Group 2 instead of struggling alone. On Sundays, I will review my progress, plan the week's tasks, and make sure I am on track before the next live class.
